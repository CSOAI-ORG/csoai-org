Mining & Mineral Extraction AI Compliance

Autonomous drilling, ore sorting, predictive maintenance, and safety systems — AI governance for the mining industry under the EU AI Act and critical raw materials regulation.

Industry Overview

Mining has embraced AI across the entire value chain — from exploration and resource estimation to extraction, processing, and environmental rehabilitation. The industry deploys computer vision systems for autonomous ore grade estimation using hyperspectral imaging; reinforcement learning for autonomous haulage vehicle routing and drill-and-blast optimisation; predictive maintenance models on IoT sensor data for crushers, conveyors, and slurry pumps; geostatistical machine learning for resource modelling and block-cave optimisation; and digital twin simulations for mine planning and ventilation management. Global mining AI spend exceeded $1.5 billion in 2025 and is projected to grow at 28% CAGR as the green energy transition drives demand for critical raw materials (lithium, cobalt, rare earth elements, copper) that require deeper, more complex extraction. Mining AI presents unique regulatory challenges due to the intersection of occupational health and safety (OHS) regulation, environmental impact assessments, community and indigenous rights, and AI governance. The EU Critical Raw Materials Act (CRMA) establishes benchmarks for domestic extraction and processing capacities, creating regulatory pressure on mining AI systems that affect exploration targeting, resource estimation accuracy, and processing plant optimisation. AI systems that control autonomous heavy machinery, monitor structural integrity of underground workings, or detect hazardous atmospheric conditions fall under the Machinery Regulation and potentially the EU AI Act's high-risk classification. The sector also faces growing scrutiny on the ethical implications of AI-driven resource allocation, community displacement modelling, and environmental impact prediction accuracy.

🇪🇺 EU AI Act Relevance

Mining AI systems engage the EU AI Act through several high-risk pathways. AI-controlled autonomous vehicles and heavy machinery (haulage trucks, drill rigs, LHD loaders) deployed in underground or surface mining operations trigger Annex III Category 2 (safety components of machinery) and the Machinery Regulation's requirements for AI-enabled safety functions. AI systems used for structural integrity monitoring of pit walls, tailings dams, and underground workings — where failure could cause catastrophic loss of life or environmental damage — may fall under Category 7 (critical infrastructure) when applied to mines classified as critical infrastructure under national law. AI-driven environmental monitoring systems that predict acid mine drainage, tailings dam stability, or groundwater contamination may trigger Category 7 when their failure could lead to major environmental incidents (directive 2004/35/EC). Article 10 (data governance) is especially pertinent — geological training data must be representative and free from systematic bias that could lead to erroneous resource estimates with significant commercial consequences. Article 14 (human oversight) requires qualified mining engineers in the loop for autonomous drilling and blasting decisions. The Article 50 transparency obligations (2 August 2026) apply to AI-generated environmental monitoring reports and community impact assessments. Cross-compliance with the Critical Raw Materials Act, Mining Waste Directive, and Seveso III Directive for chemical processing on mine sites creates a multi-layered regulatory environment where AI governance must be integrated with existing mining safety management systems. The European mining directive framework is evolving to explicitly address AI-driven autonomous mining operations.

🛡 CASA Certification Pathway

The CSOAI Mining Certification Pathway integrates AI governance with mining-specific safety and environmental regulation: Stage 1 — Mine AI Asset Inventory & Hazard Classification (identifying every AI system deployed on site — autonomous machinery, predictive maintenance, geotechnical monitoring, ventilation control, ore sorting — classified by safety criticality, EU AI Act risk tier, and CRMA nexus); Stage 2 — Autonomous Operations Safety Case (developing an AI safety case for each autonomous system, including functional safety assessment per ISO 13849/IEC 61508, human-machine interface hazard analysis, and exclusion zone validation with redundant sensor chains); Stage 3 — Technical Documentation & OHS Integration (model cards with training data provenance, bias audit for resource estimation models, explainability reports for geotechnical predictions, and integration with mine safety management systems per the Mines Regulations); Stage 4 — Environmental & Community Impact Assessment (AI bias audit for environmental monitoring models, community impact assessment transparency for AI-driven mine planning decisions, and tailings dam AI monitoring validation per Global Industry Standard on Tailings Management); Stage 5 — Continuous Monitoring & Critical Raw Materials Alignment (real-time AI model drift detection for geotechnical and environmental models, quarterly independent validation, 48-hour incident reporting for safety-critical AI failures, and annual recertification aligned with evolving CRMA benchmarks and mining safety best practice). Target timeline: 10–14 weeks for initial certification. CASA certification is recognised under mutual recognition with mining safety regulators, ICMM, and CRMA compliance pathways.
