Logistics & Supply Chain AI Compliance

Autonomous delivery, warehousing, route optimisation, and supply chain visibility — AI governance for the logistics industry under the EU AI Act and NIS2 Directive.

Industry Overview

Logistics and supply chain management has been transformed by AI across all operational layers — from strategic network design to last-metre delivery. AI applications in logistics span warehouse automation (autonomous mobile robots, robotic picking systems, AI-driven slotting optimisation); route and fleet optimisation (dynamic vehicle routing with traffic prediction, load consolidation, and real-time re-routing); demand forecasting (ensemble machine learning models for SKU-level demand prediction across multi-echelon networks); supply chain visibility and risk management (AI-powered disruption prediction, supplier risk scoring, and geopolitical event impact modelling); and autonomous delivery (AI-navigated drones, sidewalk delivery robots, and autonomous trucks). The global logistics AI market exceeded $8 billion in 2025 and is expected to reach $45 billion by 2032, driven by e-commerce growth, near-shoring trends, and regulatory pressure for supply chain transparency. Logistics operates at the intersection of multiple regulatory regimes: customs and trade compliance, transport safety regulations, food safety (cold chain), dangerous goods handling, and increasingly, AI governance. The EU's Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD) and the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) add sustainability reporting obligations that AI systems must support. AI models that make autonomous decisions about freight routing, prioritisation of shipments, or driver allocation raise concerns about algorithmic fairness, safety-critical system reliability, and transparency to supply chain partners. The NIS2 Directive extends to logistics entities designated as essential or important entities, adding cyber resilience obligations for AI systems used in supply chain management.

🇪🇺 EU AI Act Relevance

Logistics AI systems engage the EU AI Act across multiple high-risk classifications. Autonomous vehicles and mobile robots used in logistics — including autonomous trucks, warehouse AGVs, sidewalk delivery robots, and drones — trigger Annex III Category 2 (machinery safety components) under the Machinery Regulation when their navigation and obstacle avoidance systems could cause harm. AI systems used for driver scheduling, allocation, and performance monitoring in fleet operations may fall under Category 5 (employment — worker allocation decisions). AI-based customs classification and trade compliance systems that make autonomous decisions about tariff classification or export controls may trigger Category 7 (administration of justice) due to their interaction with customs enforcement. Article 10(2)(f) requires bias-free training data — critical for AI demand forecasting models that could systematically under-serve specific geographic regions or demographic segments. Article 12 (record-keeping) is especially pertinent for logistics AI — automated logging of routing decisions, driver assignments, and load allocations must be maintained for regulatory audit. Article 14 (human oversight) requires qualified transport planners in the loop for AI-driven routing changes that affect delivery commitments or safety-critical operations. The Article 50 transparency obligations (2 August 2026) apply to AI-generated tracking updates, delivery ETAs, and supply chain partner communications. Cross-compliance with the European Maritime Single Window environment, eFTI Regulation for electronic freight transport information, and the Urban Mobility Framework creates a complex regulatory environment where AI governance must be integrated with existing transport compliance systems. The CRA applies to AI-enabled logistics IoT devices and telematics systems placed on the EU market.
