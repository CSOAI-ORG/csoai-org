Network optimisation, customer service, fraud prevention, and spectrum management — AI governance for the telecommunications industry under the EU AI Act and NIS2 Directive.

Industry Overview

Telecommunications is undergoing a profound AI-driven transformation, with machine learning models now essential to network operations, customer experience, and business intelligence. Telecom operators deploy AI across five critical domains: radio access network (RAN) optimisation using reinforcement learning for dynamic spectrum allocation and beamforming; predictive maintenance of physical infrastructure (towers, fibre, base stations) using IoT sensor telemetry and anomaly detection models; customer lifecycle management with churn prediction, personalised offers, and AI-powered customer service chatbots; fraud management systems that detect SIM-box fraud, call termination bypass, and subscription fraud in real time; and 5G/6G network slicing resource allocation using deep learning for quality-of-service (QoS) optimisation. The telecommunications sector is designated as critical infrastructure under EU and national laws, making it one of the most regulated AI deployment environments. Operators must comply with the European Electronic Communications Code (EECC), NIS2 Directive (as essential entities), GDPR for call data records and location data, the EU AI Act for high-risk AI systems, and national regulatory authority (Ofcom, ANCOM, ARCEP, BNetzA) requirements. AI models that make autonomous decisions about network traffic prioritisation, service quality degradation, or access to emergency services raise particular regulatory concerns. The European Commission's White Paper on AI and the BEREC (Body of European Regulators for Electronic Communications) have both identified AI governance in telecom as a priority area, with guidance expected on AI-driven network management, customer profiling, and algorithmic fairness in service provision.

🇪🇺 EU AI Act Relevance

Telecommunications AI systems intersect with the EU AI Act through multiple high-risk classifications. AI systems used for network traffic management and resource allocation in telecom infrastructure fall under Annex III Category 7 (critical infrastructure) when their failure could disrupt essential communications services — triggering full conformity assessment (Articles 8–17, 19–22). Customer-facing AI — including chatbots, personalised pricing algorithms, and AI-driven credit scoring for contract approvals — may trigger Category 3 (access to essential services) and Category 5 (employment) when used for customer agent performance evaluation. Fraud detection AI systems that flag and block calls or subscriptions trigger Category 6 (law enforcement interface) and Category 7. Article 10(2)(f) requires bias-free training data — critical for churn prediction models that must not discriminate on age, location, or socioeconomic status. Article 14 mandates human oversight for AI-driven network management decisions that could degrade service quality or disconnect users. The Article 50 transparency obligations (effective 2 August 2026) apply to AI-generated customer communications, service recommendations, and network status notifications. Cross-compliance with the NIS2 Directive is mandatory — telecoms are essential entities with strict incident reporting (24-hour early warning, 72-hour full notification), supply chain security assessments, and management accountability requirements that directly interact with AI governance frameworks. The Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) also applies to AI-enabled telecom network equipment placed on the EU market.
