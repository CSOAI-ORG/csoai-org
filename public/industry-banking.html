Industry Overview

Banking and financial services have been at the forefront of AI adoption for decades, with machine learning now embedded in virtually every function: credit underwriting and scoring, anti-money laundering (AML) transaction monitoring, real-time fraud detection, algorithmic trading, robo-advisory wealth management, customer service chatbots, credit limit management, insurance pricing, and regulatory compliance reporting. The quantitative nature of financial data makes it uniquely suited to AI, but the sector operates under one of the heaviest regulatory burdens of any industry. Banks must comply with Basel III/IV capital requirements, IFRS 9 expected credit loss modelling standards, MiFID II best execution rules, PSD2 open banking regulations, GDPR consumer data protections, and now the EU AI Act — all while satisfying national competent authorities (FCA, BaFin, ACPR, ECB). AI models that determine creditworthiness, detect fraud, or execute trades automatically fall into high-risk categories under the EU AI Act, triggering mandatory risk management, human oversight, explainability, and bias testing requirements that interact complexly with existing model risk management frameworks like SR 11-7 and EBA GL 2023/04. The cost of non-compliance is existential — regulatory fines, model risk capital add-ons, and reputational damage that can trigger deposit outflows.

🇪🇺 EU AI Act Relevance

Banking AI systems trigger multiple high-risk classifications under Annex III. Credit scoring and mortgage affordability models fall under Category 3 (access to essential services). Fraud detection and AML models trigger Category 6 (law enforcement interface) and Category 7 (critical infrastructure) when they affect payment system integrity. Algorithmic trading systems fall under Category 7 when deployed on systemic trading venues. Insurance pricing models fall under Category 3 (access to insurance services). The EU AI Act's Article 10 (data governance) is especially critical — training data for credit models must be bias-free per Article 10(2)(f) and comply with GDPR. Article 13 (transparency) requires explanation of credit decisions to consumers, dovetailing with the Consumer Credit Directive. Article 14 (human oversight) mandates that a qualified human reviewer can override an AI credit decision. Article 50 transparency obligations (2 Aug 2026) apply to all AI-generated customer communications, investment advice, and regulatory filings. Cross-compliance with EBA model risk guidelines, MiFID II algorithmic trading requirements, and SR 11-7 is mandatory.
