Industry Overview

Maritime is undergoing its most significant technological transformation since containerisation, driven by AI-powered autonomous navigation, predictive fleet maintenance, port automation, and emissions optimisation. The International Maritime Organization (IMO) has set ambitious decarbonisation targets while the EU extends its regulatory reach through the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) for shipping, the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), and of course the EU AI Act. AI systems on vessels now manage everything from collision avoidance (COLREGS-compliant decision-support) to crew welfare monitoring, cargo stowage planning, and fuel consumption optimisation. Port terminals deploy computer vision for container tracking, AI planning for berth allocation, and autonomous quay cranes. Each of these AI systems must navigate a dense regulatory environment where maritime safety law, environmental regulation, and AI governance intersect — creating a unique compliance challenge that demands specialist certification pathways.

🇪🇺 EU AI Act Relevance

Maritime AI systems fall under multiple high-risk categories in Annex III. Autonomous navigation and collision-avoidance systems trigger Category 7 (transport safety), requiring conformity assessment under Article 43 with a Notified Body. Port biometric and surveillance systems fall under Category 1 (biometrics). Crew management and scheduling AI under Category 5 (employment). Emissions monitoring and ETS reporting AI under Category 6 (environmental protection). All high-risk maritime AI must comply with Articles 8–15 (risk management, data governance, transparency, human oversight, accuracy). The Article 50 transparency cliff (2 Aug 2026) applies to AI-generated voyage reports, emissions declarations, and crew communications. Additionally, the IMO's Maritime Safety Committee (MSC) is developing a Regulatory Scoping Exercise for MASS (Maritime Autonomous Surface Ships) that will intersect with EU AI Act requirements.

🛡 CASA Certification Pathway

The CSOAI Maritime Certification Pathway is designed for the unique regulatory stack of the shipping industry: Stage 1 — Maritime AI Audit (mapping all aboard and ashore AI systems to IMO MASS levels, EU AI Act risk categories, and flag-state requirements); Stage 2 — Safety Case Development (functional safety case per COLREGS, SOLAS, and ISM Code for each autonomous or decision-support system); Stage 3 — Technical Documentation (model card, training data (including AIS/radar/ECDIS data provenance), human-factors analysis, and fail-safe architecture); Stage 4 — Notified Body Conformity (third-party assessment coordinated with flag-state administration and recognised organisations); Stage 5 — Continuous Monitoring & Reporting (quarterly updates, incident reporting via IMO GISIS, model re-validation per SOLAS Chapter II-1). Target: 8–10 weeks for initial certification. CASA certification aligns with IMO MASS Code, EU AI Act, and classification society requirements.
