Industry Overview

Cybersecurity has become the fastest-growing AI application domain, with machine learning models powering everything from real-time intrusion detection systems (IDS) and endpoint protection platforms (EPP) to automated threat hunting, malware reverse engineering, and security orchestration automation and response (SOAR) playbooks. The global cybersecurity AI market is projected to exceed $60 billion by 2028, driven by an escalating threat landscape where AI-powered attacks (deepfake phishing, polymorphic malware, adversarial prompt injection) outpace traditional rule-based defences. Security vendors deploy AI across six primary functions: network traffic anomaly detection using autoencoders and graph neural networks; endpoint detection and response (EDR) with behavioural analysis models; cloud security posture management (CSPM) using configuration drift detection; identity and access management (IAM) with risk-based authentication; security information and event management (SIEM) correlation engines; and automated incident response triage. Cybersecurity AI operates in a uniquely adversarial environment — models must be robust against evasion attacks, data poisoning, and model extraction attempts. The EU AI Act classifies cybersecurity AI systems used for critical infrastructure protection as high-risk (Article 6, Annex III Category 7), while AI used in law enforcement cybersecurity operations falls under Category 6. NIS2 Directive imposes additional incident reporting obligations and supply chain security requirements that directly intersect with AI governance frameworks. The CISA and NCSC have both issued guidance on secure AI deployment, creating a multi-jurisdictional compliance burden for global cybersecurity vendors.

🇪🇺 EU AI Act Relevance

Cybersecurity AI systems intersect with the EU AI Act at multiple critical points. AI used for critical infrastructure protection (energy grids, water systems, financial market infrastructure, healthcare networks) is classified as high-risk under Annex III Category 7, requiring full conformity assessment including risk management (Article 9), technical documentation (Article 11), record-keeping (Article 12), transparency (Article 13), human oversight (Article 14), and accuracy/robustness (Article 15). The adversarial robustness requirements of Article 15 are especially stringent — cybersecurity models must be demonstrably resilient to evasion attacks, data poisoning, and model inversion. Article 10(5) requires that training datasets for cybersecurity AI be "relevant, representative, free from errors, and complete" — a particular challenge when training data includes adversarial samples that may themselves be biased. AI-powered perimeter defence systems that can deny physical access to infrastructure may trigger Article 5 prohibitions on certain social-scoring or real-time biometric identification under specific deployment contexts. NIS2 Directive adds mandatory incident reporting timelines (24 hours for early warning, 72 hours for full notification) that must be integrated into the AI compliance framework. The Article 50 transparency obligations (effective 2 August 2026) apply to any AI-generated threat intelligence reports or automated incident communications. Cross-compliance with the Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) for products with digital elements creates additional conformity assessment obligations for cybersecurity AI products placed on the EU market.

🛡 CASA Certification Pathway

The CSOAI Cybersecurity Certification Pathway aligns AI governance with NIS2, CRA, and EU AI Act requirements: Stage 1 — AI Asset Inventory & Criticality Assessment (cataloguing every AI system across the security stack — IDS, EDR, IAM, SOAR, CSPM — classifying each by critical infrastructure nexus and NIS2 essential/important entity status); Stage 2 — Adversarial Robustness Validation (stress-testing models against evasion, poisoning, extraction, and inversion attacks with provable robustness certificates where possible, including ART and CleverHans benchmark suites); Stage 3 — Technical Documentation & Conformity (model cards with training data provenance, adversarial training methodology, bias audit for false-positive rates across demographic groups, and human-in-the-loop escalation procedures for automated incident response); Stage 4 — NIS2/CRA Dual Compliance Mapping (mapping AI governance controls to NIS2 incident reporting obligations and CRA essential requirements for products with digital elements, with a single conformity assessment dossier accepted by all three regimes); Stage 5 — Continuous Threat Monitoring & Incident Response (real-time model drift detection against adversarial distribution shift, quarterly red-team exercises, 48-hour incident reporting to competent authorities, and annual recertification aligned with the CSOAI 52-Article Charter). Target timeline: 10–12 weeks for initial certification. CASA certification is recognised under mutual recognition agreements with CISA, NCSC, ANSSI, and BSI compliance pathways.
